This report summarizes the outputs from breakout discussions during the Modular Peer Review working group session. Participants were asked to explore potential components of modular review across several categories to begin giving form to different review purposes. The discussion centred on identifying different signals or evaluation functions associated with those components.

Each breakout group organized research components into the following conceptual review purpose categories:

- Knowledge Exchange & Sense-Making
- Improvement & Iteration
- Validity & Integrity
- Risk & Responsibility
- Decision & Resource Allocation
- Recognition & Credit
- Interoperability & Standards Alignment

Participants populated boards with examples of research objects that might be reviewed in a modular system and added notes describing the signals or evaluation purposes associated with those components.

The summaries below describe the outputs from each board.

:::{tip .dropdown} Board 1 Summary

Board 1 identified a wide range of research outputs and processes that could be evaluated through modular review, with a particular emphasis on early-stage research development and methodological components.

**Improvement & Iteration**

Participants highlighted early research components that could benefit from iterative feedback, including:

- study plans and proposals
- scripts and statistical analysis plans
- preprints
- posters
- figures and visualizations
- hypotheses and research questions
- materials availability.

Discussion notes emphasized that modular review could occur prior to traditional journal review, such as during preregistration or early research development stages. Participants also noted the importance of authors’ responses to feedback as part of iterative review.

**Validity & Integrity**

This category focused on methodological rigor and evaluation of core research elements, including:

- study design
- sampling plan
- study power calculations
- datasets and results
- raw data
- data accessibility and FAIRness.

Participants noted that traditional peer review may not fully capture multiple public “trust markers,” suggesting that modular review might enable more granular evaluation of methodological elements.

**Risk & Responsibility**

Participants associated this category with ethical oversight and responsible research practices. Components mentioned included:

- research questions
- study design
- data cleaning and analysis workflows
- statistical analysis code
- openness statements.

Discussion notes suggested that clearer frameworks or guidance for documenting research practices could support more consistent review of responsible conduct.

**Decision & Resource Allocation**

Participants identified early research planning and funding-related components that may inform resource allocation decisions, including:

- research questions and concept validation
- grant proposals and funding plans
- reagents and materials
- data collection instruments
- plans for materials (MDAR).

Notes suggested that this type of review often occurs during early stages of research planning.

**Recognition & Credit**

Participants discussed recognizing diverse research outputs beyond traditional journal articles. Examples included:

- figures and tables
- posters and talks
- preprints
- manuscripts
- datasets
- policy briefs
- review-of-reviews.

Questions were raised about how credit is assigned and how contributions across different research outputs should be recognized.

**Interoperability & Standards Alignment**

Examples in this category included:

- research software
- reagents
- genomic sequences
- protein structures
- metadata
- data collection instruments.

Participants noted that many standards remain field- or lab-specific and that clearer shared standards could improve reproducibility and comparability.

**Knowledge Exchange & Sense-Making**

Participants associated this category with communication and interpretation of research findings. Examples included:

- videos and multimedia outputs
- conceptual frameworks
- peer review reports
- post-publication commentary
- descriptions of study settings
- qualitative research materials such as interview transcripts.

Discussion notes highlighted the role of multiple formats in communicating research insights.

:::{tip .dropdown} Board 5 Summary

Board 5 surfaced conceptual refinements for the framework: clarifying what each bucket represents, distinguishing author vs reviewer perspectives, and addressing gaps such as reproducibility, replicability, and accountability.

**1. Clarify What a Sticker Represents**

Participants noted ambiguity about what placement in each bucket means. For example, when "Grant" appears in Decision & Resource Allocation, does it mean reviewing a grant proposal or using review signals to decide whether to fund? In Recognition & Credit, is the bucket about the author being credited or the reviewer being credited for evaluation work?

The framework needs clearer guidance about the perspective in each bucket: what the object is, what the review is evaluating, and how the signal is being used.

**2. Distinguish Between Author and Reviewer Perspectives**

Some review functions reflect the experience of the author being reviewed; others reflect the role of the reviewer producing signals. In Recognition & Credit, authors receive credit for research outputs while reviewers may receive credit for evaluation contributions. The framework should clarify whether each bucket represents production of research outputs or evaluation work performed by reviewers—a distinction that matters because review itself can become a recognized scholarly contribution.

**3. Clarify How Buckets Relate to One Another**

Participants noticed that Validity & Integrity, Risk & Responsibility, and Improvement & Iteration may involve similar review signals but serve different ecosystem purposes. The framework would benefit from clearer articulation of how buckets relate, where boundaries exist, and which signals appear across multiple categories—including defining cross-cutting signals versus distinct review purposes.

**4. Reproducibility and Replicability Are Missing**

Participants suggested distinguishing reproducibility (same inputs → same outputs) from replicability (hypotheses or findings hold when tested with new data or contexts). These concepts are central to scientific evaluation but do not have a clear home. The model should either add a reproducibility/replicability category or treat reproducibility as a cross-cutting signal across multiple review functions.

**5. Accountability Is Missing**

The framework does not explicitly address who is responsible for ensuring quality, how responsibility is distributed across authors, reviewers, and institutions, or how review contributes to collective accountability. A meta-level question: when research outputs and reviews accumulate, what does the combined evaluation record say about the work? The framework may need a dimension that captures accountability, stewardship of research outputs, and the collective assessment created through modular review.

**Overall Takeaway**

The exercise suggested that modular review frameworks must clarify not only what is being reviewed, but also who the signals are for, how they are used across the research ecosystem, and how responsibility and credit are distributed.

## Cross-Board Observations

Across all breakout boards, participants consistently identified a wide range of research components that could be evaluated independently through modular review.

Several recurring themes emerged across groups:

### 1\. Reviewable Components Across the Research Lifecycle

Participants identified reviewable components from multiple stages of research, including:

- early planning (research questions, study design, preregistration)
- methodological development (analysis plans, sampling plans)
- research outputs (datasets, code, figures)
- communication outputs (posters, videos, policy briefs)

:::{table #tab-lifecycle-vs-function} Research Lifecycle vs Review Function

| Stage           | Review Functions Seen        |
| --------------- | ---------------------------- |
| Idea / Question | Knowledge exchange, decision |
| Design          | Improvement, validity        |
| Data Collection | Validity, risk               |
| Analysis        | Validity, improvement        |
| Results         | Knowledge exchange           |
| Outputs         | Recognition                  |
| Infrastructure  | Standards                    |
| Governance      | Risk                         |

:::

### 2\. Modular Review Produces Different Types of Evaluation Signals

Participants associated modular review with several types of signals, including:

- methodological rigor and validity
- ethical oversight and responsible research conduct
- interoperability and standards compliance
- communication and interpretation of research findings
- recognition of contributions
- decision-making signals for funding or prioritization.

### 3\. Recognition of Diverse Research Contributions

Many groups highlighted that modular review could expand recognition beyond traditional publications to include outputs such as:

- datasets
- software and code
- protocols and methods
- peer review reports
- communication outputs

### 4\. Overlap Between Evaluation Categories

Participants frequently noted potential overlap between categories, particularly between:

- validity and risk
- decision-making and risk evaluation
- improvement and knowledge exchange.

These overlaps suggest that different evaluation functions may operate simultaneously when reviewing a research component.

### 5\. Importance of Standards and Documentation

Several groups emphasized that standards, metadata, and documentation play an important role in **enabling reuse and interoperability of research outputs**.

## Summary

The breakout discussions produced a broad set of examples of research components that could be evaluated with modular peer review. Participants identified potential evaluation functions spanning research design, methods, communication, ethics, and infrastructure.

Across boards, participants emphasized that modular review could allow different research components to be evaluated independently, yielding multiple signals on research quality, transparency, and impact.

Across breakout groups, several research components appeared repeatedly across multiple review functions, particularly study design, datasets, code, and protocols, suggesting these elements may serve as central objects within modular peer review systems.

These discussions illustrate a wide range of possibilities for how modular peer review could support evaluation throughout the research lifecycle.
