Participants were asked to place dots on research objects or processes where modular peer review could be most valuable. The goal was not to select a single winner but to identify areas of shared interest and momentum across groups, several patterns emerged.

## Patterns

**Strong Convergence Around Upstream Research Design**

The clearest signal was preregistration, which received the most votes overall (4). Related elements, such as study design and study power analysis, were also mentioned across groups. This indicates a strong interest in applying modular review earlier in the research lifecycle, before results are finalized.

**Strong Interest in Data**

Multiple groups highlighted different aspects of data. These included:

- raw data
- data accessibility
- general data references

While different, these suggestions all pointed to the importance of reviewing data objects themselves, including their accessibility and structure or metadata.

**Technical Reproducibility Components**

A smaller but consistent cluster focused on code and computational workflows, including code repositories, notebooks, and analysis workflows. These were framed as key components supporting reproducibility and transparency.

**Infrastructure and Incentive Signals**

Several votes pointed toward broader research infrastructure, including grants and funding processes and research integrity checks. These reflect interest in exploring how modular review might intersect with existing evaluation and governance systems.

**Emerging Questions Around AI Usage**

There were also three votes in favour of AI use in research. While this topic did not become a standalone working track, it signals an important cross-cutting issue. Each working subgroup is expected to consider how AI tools intersect with their topic area, including implications for transparency, documentation, and review.

**Communication and Interpretation of Results**

Some suggestions related to how research findings are communicated and interpreted, including:

- figures and data visualizations
- discussion sections
- interpretation of main claims

These ideas highlight the importance of improving the clarity and interpretability of research outputs.

## Selection of Working Sub-Groups

Following the dot-voting exercise, the organizing team synthesized the results to identify five working sub-groups for deeper exploration through June.

These groups were selected based on three criteria:

- Vote strength, reflecting the areas of greatest interest across participants
- Clustering of related ideas that emerged across multiple groups
- Coverage across the research lifecycle, ensuring a range of objects serving different purposes and audiences were included

Because many suggestions overlapped conceptually, related items were grouped into broader working areas where applicable.

**1\. Preregistration**

Including:

- study design
- study power analysis

This track reflects a substantial interest in exploring modular review earlier in the research lifecycle, before research is conducted.

**2\. Data**

Including:

- raw data
- data accessibility
- related data references

This group will explore how datasets themselves could be reviewed, including questions of data quality, documentation, accessibility, and reuse.

**3\. Code & Notebooks**

Including:

- code repositories
- computational notebooks
- code / Notebook workflows

This area focuses on improving computational reproducibility through review of analytical workflows and research software.

**4\. Funding & Grant Proposals**

Including:

- grant applications
- funding applications
- funding plans

This track explores how elements of modular review could potentially support funding evaluation processes.

**5\. Figures & Visualizations**

Including:

- figures
- data visualizations

This group focuses on improving the clarity and interpretability of research outputs and on the role of review in strengthening scientific communication.

## Cross-Group Considerations

While the five sub-groups provide the primary structure for deeper work, AI usage in research emerged as an important cross-cutting theme. Each subgroup is encouraged to consider how AI tools intersect with their area of focus, including implications for transparency, documentation, and review practices.

The discussion also raised broader questions about how review should function when research objects are reused. As items such as data, figures, and code move across repositories, publications, and new research contexts, there is a need to think more carefully about how reviews travel with them, if they should travel at all, and how they remain visible and meaningful in different settings.

Participants also highlighted the importance of exploring how new reviews might build on earlier ones, how prior assessments should be interpreted in new contexts, and whether different forms of reuse require different types of review. These questions will likely surface across several subgroups as the work progresses.
